About the Municipality

Sibonga, Cebu

Sibonga is a 3rd class municipality located in the southern part of Cebu province in the Philippines. It is situated along the southwestern coast of Cebu Island, facing the Tañon Strait that separates Cebu from Negros Island.

The municipality is part of the 3rd District of Cebu and is bounded by the municipalities of Argao to the south, Carcar City to the north, Alegria to the southwest, and the Tañon Strait to the west.

Sibonga is home to the world-famous Simala Shrine — one of the Philippines' most visited pilgrimage destinations — while its coastal communities continue a proud tradition of fishing and agriculture.

Heritage

History of Sibonga

Pre-Colonial Era

Early Settlements

Indigenous Visayan communities inhabited the coastal areas of what is now Sibonga long before Spanish colonization. These early settlers relied on fishing in the Tañon Strait and farming in the interior upland areas for their livelihood.

1565 – 1600s

Spanish Colonization

With the arrival of Spanish colonizers in Cebu under Miguel Lopez de Legazpi, the region was gradually brought under colonial administration. The Catholic faith was introduced and a parish was established, profoundly shaping the culture and traditions that persist to this day.

17th – 18th Century

Establishment as a Municipality

Sibonga was formally constituted as a municipality during the Spanish colonial period. The town center developed around the church and plaza, following the typical colonial urban design pattern of Philippine municipalities.

1898 – 1946

American Colonial Period and World War II

During the American period, public schools were established and local governance was reorganized. Like much of Cebu, Sibonga experienced the hardships of the Japanese occupation during World War II before the liberation of the Philippines in 1945.

1946 – Present

Post-Independence Growth

Following Philippine independence, Sibonga continued to develop as a municipality within Cebu province. The Simala Shrine has transformed Barangay Simala into one of the country's most famous pilgrimage destinations, bringing widespread recognition to the municipality.

Tourism

Places to Visit

Simala Shrine
Pilgrimage

Simala Shrine (Our Lady of Simala)

One of the Philippines' most visited pilgrimage destinations, the castle-like Simala Shrine in Barangay Simala is dedicated to Our Lady of Simala. Thousands of devotees arrive each year seeking healing and miracles from the miraculous image of the Virgin Mary.

Tañon Strait
Nature

Tañon Strait Coastline

The western coast of Sibonga faces the scenic Tañon Strait, a protected seascape between Cebu and Negros Island. Visitors can enjoy fishing, coastal walks, and breathtaking sunset views over the open water.

Parish Church
Heritage

Saint Michael the Archangel Parish Church

The centuries-old parish church stands as the spiritual and historical center of Sibonga. Built during the Spanish colonial era, this heritage structure remains an active place of worship and an enduring symbol of Sibonga's faith.

Fishing Communities
Coastal

Fishing Communities and Coves

The coastal barangays host traditional fishing communities where visitors can observe traditional bangka boat-building, watch fishermen at work, and sample fresh seafood caught daily from the Tañon Strait.

Town Center & Plaza
Culture

Sibonga Town Center and Plaza

The town plaza reflects the traditional colonial layout of Philippine municipalities. This social heart of the town comes alive during local fiestas, community events, and lively weekend markets throughout the year.

Upland Views
Nature

Upland Farms and Scenic Views

The interior upland barangays of Sibonga offer lush agricultural landscapes, fresh cool air, and sweeping views of the Cebu highlands — a peaceful escape from the coast and a glimpse of traditional rural Filipino life.

Traditions

Culture and Festivals

Feast of Saint Michael the Archangel

The main town fiesta honoring the patron saint features processions, novenas, cultural shows, and community gatherings that unite residents from all 44 barangays.

Simala Marian Devotion

The Simala Shrine hosts major celebrations on Marian feast days. Devotees from across the Philippines make the pilgrimage to Sibonga year-round to venerate Our Lady of Simala.

Cebuano Heritage and Language

The people of Sibonga speak Cebuano (Bisaya) and take deep pride in their Visayan roots, preserving traditional music, dance, and craftsmanship through schools and community groups.

Traditional Fishing Practices

Coastal communities maintain fishing methods passed down through generations, including bangka boats and local net and line techniques suited to the Tañon Strait's waters.

Local Cuisine

Fresh seafood, rice-based meals, kinilaw (raw fish cured in vinegar), sinuglaw, and dried fish are staples. The food culture reflects both the coastal and agricultural character of the municipality.

Barangay Fiestas

Each of the 44 barangays celebrates its patron saint's feast day with neighborhood gatherings, shared meals, games, and religious observances — strengthening bonds within each community.

Geography

Location and How to Get There

Sibonga is located in the southern portion of Cebu Island, approximately 73 kilometers south of Cebu City, along the southwestern coast bordered by the Tañon Strait.

Bordered By

  • North: Carcar City
  • South: Argao
  • East: Alegria (upland area)
  • West: Tañon Strait (facing Negros Island)

Coordinates

Approximately 10.0333 N, 123.5667 E

How to Get There

  • From Cebu City: Take a bus or van bound for Oslob or Bato from the South Bus Terminal. Travel time is approximately 1.5 to 2 hours.
  • Via private vehicle: Follow the national highway south through Talisay, Minglanilla, Naga, Carcar, and into Sibonga.
  • Habal-habal (motorcycle taxi) and tricycles are available within the municipality for local transport.

Livelihood

Economy and Industry

The economy of Sibonga is anchored in agriculture, fishing, and increasingly, religious tourism driven by the Simala Shrine.

Agriculture

Corn, root crops, coconut, and vegetable farming in upland barangays

Fishing

Small-scale commercial and subsistence fishing in the Tañon Strait

Religious Tourism

Simala Shrine draws pilgrims and tourists from across the country year-round

Commerce

Local markets, sari-sari stores, and small business establishments
